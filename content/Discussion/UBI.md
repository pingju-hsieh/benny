**Field:** Industrial Organization / Contract Theory

**Research Question** In a market characterized by vertical separation between data owners (OEMs) and risk carriers (Insurers), how do dynamic information frictions and double marginalization distort the adoption of Usage-Based Insurance (UBI)? Furthermore, what is the optimal regulatory mechanism—whether through subsidies, data portability mandates, or contract regulation—that a Social Planner should implement to internalize the massive positive externalities of safer driving?

**Motivation: The "Win-Win-Win" Promise of Digitized Contracts** 

The digitization of individual behavior—specifically through telematics in the automotive sector—offers a theoretically perfect mechanism to align private incentives with social welfare. In an ideal Usage-Based Insurance (UBI) market, the value creation is threefold:

1. **For Consumers:** Safer drivers signal their low-risk type, unlocking fair pricing and reducing their cost of living.
2. **For Insurers:** Granular data resolves asymmetric information problems, allowing for precise screening and better risk segmentation.
3. **For Society:** The mechanism acts as a "nudge" (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008), incentivizing safer driving habits and reducing the massive external costs of congestion and accidents.

Recent empirical evidence suggests that monitoring can reduce accident probabilities by nearly **30%** (Jin & Vasserman, 2021). If successful, this narrative extends beyond transportation; it represents a generalizable framework for any "behavior-based contract," such as interactive health insurance where premiums adjust to physical activity.

**The Economic Puzzle: Market Failure and Vertical Frictions**

Despite this immense potential, the market is failing to clear. Current adoption of UBI remains historically low (approx. 17%), and traditional insurers are increasingly pulling back from these offerings due to fragile revenue margins. Simultaneously, Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) like Tesla are attempting to enter the insurance market directly, yet they too struggle to find the optimal pricing strategy in a vertically disintegrated market. I hypothesize that this stagnation is driven by the interaction of three distinct failures:

- **The Unpriced Externality:** As noted by Parry et al. (2007), the external cost of driving (congestion, public health) far exceeds the private cost. Private firms lack the profit motive to incentivize the socially optimal level of adoption.
- **Vertical Data Frictions:** A structural conflict exists where OEMs act as "data gatekeepers," charging access fees that prevent downstream insurers from offering sufficient privacy discounts.
- **The Ratchet Effect:** Consumers fear that revealing their "safe" type today will destroy their informational rent in the future (Laffont & Tirole, 1988), creating a dynamic disincentive to share data.

**Research Agenda** To address these interconnected failures, I aim to construct a structural model of the UBI market featuring four strategic agents: the Consumer, the Insurer, the OEM, and the Social Planner. I will try to construct the model from the simplest one, and add more and more characteristic on it. By explicitly modeling the vertical constraints and dynamic incentives, I will simulate counterfactual regulatory policies to determine how to unlock this market. Specifically, I aim to analyze whether mandating data portability between OEMs and insurers can break the vertical bottleneck, and how a planner can design a subsidy that incentivizes adoption with less value being captured by the data monopolist.
